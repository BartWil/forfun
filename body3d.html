The Anatomy

A 3D articulated figure whose hips, knees, and ankles move on the same literature-grounded joint-angle curves as every other page — and whose muscles glow from cool to hot with their real activation levels. Orbit around it, scrub the cycle, and watch which muscles do the work through the movement. Built in three.js (WebGL) running straight in your browser.

This build uses stylised procedural bone & muscle volumes as a stand-in. The rendering and data pipeline is designed so a real anatomical mesh (e.g. an open Z-Anatomy / BodyParts3D model) can be dropped in later without changing the data wiring.

What you're looking at

The % is one stride, not seconds

Every curve runs left-to-right over one full gait cycle — from one foot's ground contact to that same foot's next contact — rescaled to 0–100 % so different people and speeds line up. The playhead % at the top is where in that cycle you are now.

Stance ~0–39% foot on the ground
Swing ~39–100% foot in the air

For running the foot is on the ground only ~39% of the stride (the rest is swing, including brief flight) — that split is measured from this very data set and is where the muscle bursts are timed against.

Colour = how hard a muscle is working for it

Each muscle's signal is normalised to its own peak in the cycle: 0 = quiet, 1.0 = that muscle's hardest moment. So colour tells you when a muscle fires, not whether the calf is “stronger” than the quad — cross-muscle amplitude from surface EMG isn't comparable, so we don't imply it.

What's measured vs. what's modelled

SignalMovementWhere it comes from
Muscle activationRunning Real EMG. Grand average of 11,388 gait cycles from 135 adults (Santuz et al. 2018, open data)
Muscle activationWalk, jump, land, squat Illustrative reconstructions from the biomechanics literature
Joint angles & ground forceAll Literature-based reconstructions (Perry, Winter, Novacheck, …)

How the running EMG was turned into these curves: raw signals → 50 Hz high-pass → full-wave rectify → 20 Hz low-pass envelope → normalise each muscle to its own peak → time-normalise every cycle to 100 stance + 100 swing points → average across all 11,388 cycles. This is the standard pipeline from the paper. The 13 recorded channels are pooled into the five shown here (e.g. rectus femoris + both vasti → “Quadriceps”).

Both views read the same numbers at each instant. The 2D map is the open body-muscles library (Apache 2.0); the 3D figure is three.js. Educational visualisation — not a clinical or individual assessment.
