What you're looking at

The motion is measured, not animated by hand

The hip, knee, and ankle rotations come from the Fukuchi et al. 2018 open walking data set — 3D motion capture of 42 healthy adults walking overground at a comfortable speed. We took the grand-average sagittal joint angles (averaged over every subject and both legs, 84 gait curves) and retargeted them straight onto the skeleton. Nothing here is a canned game animation.

Stance ~0–62% foot on the ground
Swing ~62–100% foot in the air

The timeline is one gait cycle — one foot's ground contact to that same foot's next contact — rescaled to 0–100%. In walking the foot is down ~62% of the cycle (there's no flight phase, unlike running). The other leg runs exactly half a cycle behind.

How a body walks from angles alone

Joint angles say how the limbs bend but not where the body is in space — that pelvis motion isn't in the angle data. So the walking is reconstructed with foot-locking: the foot in contact is pinned to the ground and the body is carried over it, which recovers real overground progression (≈0.8 m per stride here). The camera rides along, so the figure looks in place while the ground slides past and the feet stay genuinely planted.

The body itself is a generic rigged mannequin (three.js “Xbot”). The data driving it is real; the mesh is just the canvas. Sagittal plane only — the measured out-of-plane rotations are small and left out for clarity.

Data: Fukuchi CA, Fukuchi RK, Duarte M (2018), A public data set of overground and treadmill walking kinematics and kinetics of healthy individuals, PeerJ 6:e4640 / figshare 5722711. Rendering: three.js. Educational visualisation — not a clinical or individual assessment.
